What is Speleometrics?

Speleometrics aims to create a centralized, transparent platform for gathering and sharing cave metrics specific to the Quadrilátero Ferrífero region. This platform will serve various stakeholders—speleologists, conservationists, environmental consultancy companies, public agencies, academics, and cave enthusiasts—by providing accessible and accurate data on caves. The user-friendly dashboard will offer insights into key cave attributes (such as length, depth, area, volume and others), helping in decision-making for environmental licensing, conservation, and research.

By allowing input, updates, and the display of cave data, Speleometrics aims to support informed decisions on the preservation of speleological heritage in a region where mining and environmental conservation are often in conflict. The platform will ensure that cave metrics are continually updated, making them easily accessible and reliable for all stakeholders.

What is the importance of cave metrics in environmental licensing and conservation?

To answer this question, we first need to review the legislation related to this topic. Cave legislation in Brazil has evolved over the last thirty years, primarily driven by the conflict between increasing economic pressure on natural resources and the growing concern for environmental preservation and conservation. In this context, there is a need to preserve and conserve speleological heritage in the face of impactful activities, such as those related to the mining sector.

The 1988 Brazilian Constitution establishes that natural underground cavities in Brazil are assets of the Union. Depending on their characteristics, they can also be considered sites of historical, scenic, artistic, archaeological, paleontological, ecological, and scientific value. Another aspect of the legislation concerns the requirement for licensing projects and activities that may potentially or effectively pollute or degrade caves and their area of influence.

Thus, it was necessary to establish degrees of relevance for caves to reconcile projects and speleological heritage. Within the scope of environmental licensing, caves must be classified according to their degree of relevance as maximum, high, medium, or low. This classification is based on an analysis of ecological, biological, geological, hydrological, paleontological, scenic, historical-cultural, and socioeconomic attributes, evaluated from both a regional and local perspective, according to criteria and methodologies established by specific legislation. It is noteworthy that metric factors are predominant and are evaluated on both local (Geomorphological Unit) and regional (Speleological Unit, in this case, the Quadrilátero Ferrífero) scales.

The final classification of a cave's relevance determines whether irreversible negative impacts (suppression) on natural cavities are permissible. Caves classified as having maximum relevance cannot be subjected to irreversible negative impacts. For other relevance levels—high, medium, and low—irreversible negative impacts are allowed, provided specific compensation measures are adopted for high and medium levels. For caves with low relevance, no compensation is required.

In light of this context, a comprehensive and unbiased data platform, like Speleometrics, is an essential tool for decision-making in the early stages of a project and for comparing the data adopted by stakeholders in studies.

What is considered a cave?

According to Brazilian legislation, a natural underground cavity is defined as "any underground spaces penetrable by humans, with or without an identifiable opening, [...] including its environment, its mineral and water content, the biotic communities found there, and the rock body in which it is embedded, provided that its formation was due to natural processes, regardless of its dimensions or the type of host rock." It is important to note that caves are part of a karstic system/landscape, representing what is conventionally referred to as the endokarst.

What is the Quadrilátero Ferrífero?

The term Quadrilátero Ferrífero was introduced by Dorr (1969) and collaborators during mapping of the region conducted by the U.S. Geological Survey (USGS) to describe the landscape of lush hills and valleys south of the city of Belo Horizonte, in the state of Minas Gerais, covering about 15,000 km². The term is credited to the abundance of high-grade iron ore bodies in the region and its roughly quadrangular shape, with a loose translation being "Iron Quadrangle."

The Quadrilátero Ferrífero is Brazil's main iron ore-producing province, and the iron formations exposed there belong to a Paleoproterozoic platform sequence (~2.4 billion years old). In this region, the iron formations are called itabirito, a banded iron formation named by the Indigenous people (Tupi-Guarani). Additionally, ferruginous crusts known as canga, also named by the Indigenous people, occur in the area. It is important to highlight that the same iron enrichment process—due to leaching over geological eras—results in the relative concentration of iron, and also creates voids through a process known as piping, which develop into caves. Consequently, there is a conflict between the region's main economic activity, iron mining, and the conservation of speleological heritage.

Why should I contribute?

The continuous provision of data to the site ensures that speleological metrics are constantly updated, reflecting a more accurate overall picture of the Quadrilátero Ferrífero, which in turn contributes to clearer criteria for cave relevance and the proper management of speleological heritage.
